The Diagnosis

At one a'clock in the afternoon of March 20th, 1345, the planets Mars and Jupiter entered the sign of Aquarius and began to interact upon each other and the planet Earth. Jupiter,the warm,moist planet, drew up evil vapours: Mars, the hot, dry planet, set them alight. The heavens became disturbed as the constellations, by divine command, fought to aid the inhabitants of Earth. Flashes of brilliant light, the swooping passage of comets,balls of fire, fiery beams of wood- all these signified to the wise the advent of a vast calamity. In the Great Sea near India the waters boiled and heaved and a stinking mist arose which, drifting across the land, brought death with it. Reptiles and frogs were bred from the corruption and, in turn, spread contagion." That was one of the theries that some people thought. It was also believed that the series of violant earthquakes that disturbed southern Europe in the mid-14th century contributed to the plague. The foul vapours engendered by Jupiter were supposed to have built up inside the Earth and eventually forced their way out, creating earthquakes and adding there quota of poison to the atmosphere. The astrological theory was supposed to explain the remote cause of plague. Everyone agree that the immediate cause was the corruption of the air, but physicians differed as to how the corruption was actually brought about. Some held that it was produced by the huge swarms of locusts that periodically descended upon Europe from the East. After stripping an area of its vegitation , the insects would die by the million and thererotting corpses were supposed to contribute to the " foul vapours" swirling over the Earth. The theory was a clear example of the confusion of the cause and effect. The Plague did frequently follow the locust invasions, but the link was not direct. The locust destroyed the crops, thereby creating widespread famine which , in turn, lowered the general resistance to the disease. Other physicians believed that the very elements of the air could not decay and so breed poison, or that the corruption was produced by change of seasons, by violant hot winds, or by the movements of animals. Curiously, many observers noticed the relationship between plague and rats which has now been proved to be part of the plague cycle. In the late 19th century it was established that the plague developed in the bodies of the fleas which, in thier turn, were parasites upon rats. As early ad the 10th century, however, the Arabian physician Avicenna actualy recorded that it was a sure sign of pestilence when rats came out of there holes and died in the open. If the rats had been examined it would have been seen that they were swarming with fleas, which passed to human hosts as soon as the rats were dead. But the warning passed unnoticed, buried among the scores of fantastic theories. Flea-bites were common human afflications, and there was no particular reason why people should suspect one class of flea rather than another. The term " Black Death" which we now use to describe this great epidemic came into use only in the 17th century. It describes the appearence of the body after death, when haemorrhages under ths skin gave the corpse a dark, mottled hue. In England, the term was probably adopted in order to make a distinction between earlyer plagues and the "Great Plague" which struck London in 1665. Even the word "plague" only came into use long after the great outbreak of the mid-14th century. Wycliffe, translating the Bible into English in 1382, used the word "plague" for a Latin word meaning blow or stroke. It was a vivid description of the terible speed with which the disease could act and it immediately passed into general use. The physicians in that time wrote down the two types of the plague, the first lasted two months, with constant fever and blood-spiting of this people died in 3 days. The second one lasted for the rest of the time. In this, together with constant fever, there were external carbuncles, or buboes, under the arm or in the groin, and the disease ran its course in five days. The contagion was great especially when there was blood-spitting, that even by looking at the sick it seemed people got it. The first pneumonic plague, was the more deadly, for it affected the respiratory system: "It is found that all who have died thus have had their lungs infected." The second, or bubonic, gave the victim some chance to recover. When people found that the docter was helpless they turned to the magician, who was swift to oblige-at a price. The Economic Effect

One of the most dramatic monuments to the effect of the Black Death is the cathedral of Siena in Italy, originally it was planned to be even largerthan St Peter's in Rome, the largest church in Europe, but work had barely strarted when the plague struck Siena in 1348. The death roll mounted into the tens of thousands and all work was halred; but even when normality returned it was not possible to contunue building on the same scale. The vast sum of money set aside for the cathedral had to be employed for the more pressing purposes of health. When work recommenced, the builders ignored the foundations and was that had already been raised and started all over. Throughout Europe there exists this evidence of "work suspended". There had beed other terrible epidemics which took their thousands, but they had been comparatively localized, affecting only a single country or even a single city. The Black Death, gripping the entire continent in one brief spell, effectivily broke the thred of history. The major cause of changevwas the heavy labour loss which resulted from the vast death roll- but a true calculation of that loss is today impossible. Medieval statistics are notoriously misleading: distance, time, quantity- all are measured only by the roughest approximation. Figure given for populations are therefore vague in the extreme. There was no system for registering either births or deaths, neither was their anything that corresponds to modern census-taking. Estimates of population, when required, were made by the rule-of-thumb methods: the proportion of able-bodied men liable for military service, or the tax produced by the consumption of somevcommodity, might be used to reach a round figure. The only reasonably accurate method of calculation is that based on the records of universities and ecclesiastical offices. The Dance of Death

physicians might examine corpses and astrologers plot the movements of planets to acount for the physical origins of the plague, but to the vast mass of people, there was only one true cause- the wrath of God. The plague was a divine punishment for sin. The fact was driven home in countless sermons: the wearing of pointed shoes, drinking, dancing, gambling, attending the theatre - all these, at one time or another, were cited as rousing God's especial anger. Sometimes the weight of public opinion could force a whole trade out of existence: the makers of gambling dice survived only by turning their skills to the makeing of rosary beads. Superstitious terrors created fantastic stories. Terrified people swore that they had seen a spirit of evil who spread the plague. It sometimes appeared sometimes in the form of blue smoke emerging from the mouth of a dead person. At other times it looked like a beautiful woman who rode the winds casting pestilence like a sower of seed: the Germans called her the plague Maiden. A vivid legend arose in Rome. "Many saw with their own eyes, a good and bad angel would indicate a door, whereupon the bad angel would strike it with a great lance, each blow sighifying the death of one person in the household." Death himself was said to have appeared in human form and from this legend arose that great series of pictures known as the "Dance of Death" which, more than anything else, shows how the sick fear of death gripped the entire continent. All over Europe there appeared this figure of Death, sometimes crudely chalked up on a barn or church walls, sometimes beautifully painted. Some of the greatest artists of the day would creat some of those and also show death doing something. The most widespread soperstition was the belief that the plague was caused by invivible arrows hurled by Christ. The church taught that Christ's anger could be turned aside by his mothers pleas and the Virgin Mary therefore appeared in a dominant position on the plague banners which were carried in processions. Precessions were usually under the auspices of the church, the penitents walking in a orderly fashion from one shrine to another. But at the height of the Black Death this decorous ritual took a horrifying turn in the processions of the Flagellants. The brotherhood of the Cross, as they called themselves arose in Hungary and created an order, complete with regulations and uniform, out of the formless and spontaneous wandering of homeless people. "They were robed in sombre garments with red crosses on the breast, back and on the cap and bore triple scourges [wips] tied in three knots in which points of iron were fixed." On arrival at a town, they handed the citizens a copy of a remarkable letter from Jesus Christ which they claimed had fallen from heaven. It set forth a horrific programme which God had devised for the punishment of man. The punishment could be avoided if the example of the Flagellants was followed. Each procession was to last for 33.5 days- one day for every year of Christ's life. At every plave they visited, "they entered the church and closed the door and each one divested himself of his upper clothing. Then marching in procession around the church, each scourged himself until the blood ran down over his ankles." The movement spread from Hungary throughout eastern Europe, crossing at last into England in the autumm of 1349. England had not yet suffered the full terrors of the plague and the fanatics were expelled after they had created a disturbance in London. Throughout Europe authorities became alarmed, for the bands, many thousands strong, began to threaten what remained of organized goverment. They were excommunicated and threatened with execution. Gradually, the bands broke up; but under different names, they were to appear again many times. Then the people turned againsed the Jews who they blamed. The first major outbreak against the Jews took place at Chillon, near Geneva, in the autumm of 1348. It was dignified by the name of "trial", for the local Jews were accused of poisoning wells and were brought before the magistrates. Afterwards, in defence of their action, the people of Chillon published an account of the "trial" and it shows cleary how the resulting massacre developed. Under pain of torture a Jewish physician "confessed" that he had placed poison in a well and named those who have given the poison to him. Each of these in turn were tortured, implicating yetmore until scores were involved. All were burnt. Not all public authorities were so stupid or so cynical. The magistrates of Cologne said firmly:" We consider the morality to be a scourge of God and consequently we shall not permit prosecutionsof Jews in our towns." Sensibly they went on to point out that, ounce started, mass murder could not be controlled and Christians as well as Jews would suffer at the hands of the rabble-rousers. But it was too late to arrest the process and other cities followed the example of Chillon, not of Cologne. In Strasbourg, 2,000 Jews were burnt on a great scanold: in Basle hundreds more were forced into a wooden building which was set alight. In vain, the Pope threatened excommunication for all those who took part in the persecutions. France, Germany, Switzerland, Holland, England, all joined in the orgy of cruelty. In Avignon alone was there refuge, for the Pope was able to place the Jews there under his direct protection. In the early stages of the epidemic, the decencies of burial were observed with individual coffins for the corpses and all the rites if the church. But as the death roll increased, so mass burials in vast pits became the norm. "The dead outnumber the living" was a common cry and the most desperate measures were adopted in order to keep a city reasonably clear of corpses. It was now that the grave-diggers came into their own, for they were indispensable. They were drawn from the dregs of society-ex-convicts, galley slaves-men who had nothing to lose but hoped to gain pickings by robbing the dead or blackmailing the living. Part of there work was to remove the sick from private homes to plague-houses. But,"every definite case of plague is incurable," an Italian physician had pronounced and it was true. The plague-house was not a place to which a sick person went in hope but rather a waiting-room for the grave to which he was dragged. He would therefore be prepared to pay handsomely not to be taken away, and the grave-digger, pocketing his ransom, would go elsewhere. The span of the average grave-digger's life was brief-although probably not much briefer than his span of life as a galley-slave. While he lived, he could exercise a brutal power over the un important citizens who were forced to remain in the city. So feared and hated was he that, in some countries, he was known simply by the terrible name of " Death".
